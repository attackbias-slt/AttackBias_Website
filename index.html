Not All Attacks are Learned
Equally in Speech Deepfake Detection

Abstract Method Results Code & Models ↗

Abstract

Speech deepfake detection (SDD) models are trained on multi-attack datasets containing diverse spoofing systems, such as text-to-speech (TTS) and voice conversion (VC). In standard multi-attack training, all attacks are treated as one spoof class, and performance is reported using overall Equal Error Rate (EER). This aggregate view obscures how individual attacks shape learning and generalization. To better understand this attack-level behavior, we first balance TTS and VC exposure using sample and attack omission. We then measure attack-wise EER at inference and analyze attack-wise training loss and predictive entropy to characterize optimization. Results show that attacks contribute unequally: some attacks have high EER sensitivity and concentrated entropy with low loss, indicating strong influence on the decision boundary. We define these as high-impact attacks. To reduce uneven generalization across attacks, we propose a replay-regularized, attack-aware curriculum that stages exposure based on measured attack influence. Experiments on ASVspoof 2019, 2021, ASVspoof 5, and Fake-or-Real show improved overall robustness and reduced attack-level imbalance compared with standard multi-attack training.

Method

We characterize each attack type along two complementary axes using an SLS-XLSR backbone: EER sensitivity (leave-two-attacks-out EER change) and predictive entropy of model output distributions. Code and pretrained models are available at github.com/attackbias-slt/Paper_Codes ↗.

Figure 1. Impact analysis pipeline. EER sensitivity and entropy analysis applied to SDD benchmark datasets yield a characterization of high-impact attacks (concentrated entropy) versus low-impact attacks (diffused entropy).

Armed with this partition, we train a two-stage curriculum. Stage 1 pretrains on low-impact attacks (60% subset), building representations for the harder-to-learn attacks before exposure to high-impact gradients. Stage 2 adapts using a composite loss:

Lstage2 = αLhigh + αrLrep + βLKD + γLcon

Figure 2. Two-stage training framework. Stage 1 pretrains on low-impact attacks. Stage 2 adapts to high-impact attacks with a frozen teacher providing knowledge distillation and contrastive regularization via a 40% low-impact replay subset.

Results & Analysis

We report Equal Error Rate (EER %, ↓) for standard multi-attack training and impact-driven curriculum variants trained on ASVspoof 2019 LA.

High-Impact vs. Low-Impact Attacks

High-Impact Attacks
High EER sensitivity — omitting these attacks causes large EER swings, indicating strong influence on the decision boundary
Concentrated entropy — model outputs are confident and peaked, suggesting discriminative features have been learned for these attacks
Low-Impact Attacks
Low EER sensitivity — omitting these attacks has minimal effect on overall EER, despite their presence in training data
Diffused entropy — model outputs remain uncertain, indicating these attacks are underrepresented in learned representations

Key Findings

Impact Asymmetry

A small subset of attack types dominates gradient updates. High-impact attacks exhibit concentrated entropy, while low-impact attacks show diffused entropy despite frequent co-training.

Curriculum Ordering

Pretraining on low-impact attacks before high-impact exposure prevents representation collapse and yields more robust cross-dataset generalization.

Replay Prevents Forgetting

Without the replay loss Lrep in Stage 2, performance on low-impact attacks degrades substantially, confirming catastrophic forgetting under high-impact fine-tuning.

Cross-Dataset Gains

EER improvements generalize across ASVspoof 2019, 2021, ASVspoof 5, and Fake-or-Real, suggesting bias mitigation yields representations that transfer better across spoofing scenarios.
